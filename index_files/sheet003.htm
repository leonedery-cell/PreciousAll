                  LBMA Vaults Data / Gold - Aug 2025                    
                  The amount of gold held in the London vaults at the end of August decreased by 0.3m ounces month on month to 284.2m ounces. Of these, around 167.3m ounces are holdings at the BOE and 83m ounces are earmarked for ETFs and other private holders (figure calculated via own ventilation of all existing ETFs custodian vault location).
In effect, the 'free-float' of gold in London's vaults comes at a net 33.9m ounces, down by 3.1m ounces from the previous month.
👉 ETFs and custodial holdings grew sharply last month, with demand adding 3.5m ounces and siphoning off liquidity.

In contrast, the LBMA OTC Trade Data shows that an average of 38.1m ounces gold changed hands every day in the week to Sep-08. This suggests a surplus of available unallocated gold with the free-float effectively running a positive balance of 4.1m ounces.

        ANALYZING THE FLOAT
 
             
                                 
                  London's free float currently covers less than 1 day of OTC spot trading, it's a bit tight and we think that premium for gold in other trading hubs are sucking gold out of London                
                                 
                                 
                                 
                  Since 2020, the float has lifted from ~8% to ~14% of vault holdings. Yet this still equates to only 1 to 2 days of trading cover. Gold's liquidity is an illusion of velocity: turnover looks deep, but the true cushion of mobilizable metal remains razor-thin.                
                                 
                                 
                                 
                                 
                  LBMA clearing data shows the float covers average daily settlements (~16.3m oz - August figures). But in a stress scenario, where investors demand physical allocation, the float would be exhausted in around 2 settlement days, underscoring how fragile gold's market structure remains beneath the surface.
