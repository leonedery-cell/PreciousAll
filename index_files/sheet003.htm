                  The amount of gold held in the London vaults at the end of July decreased by 2.9m ounces month on month to 285m ounces. Of these, around 167.9m ounces are holdings at the BOE and78.6m ounces are earmarked for ETFs and other private holders (figure calculated via own ventilation of all existing ETFs custodian vault location).
In effect, the 'free-float' of gold in London's vaults comes at a net 38.5m ounces, down by 0.7m ounces from the previous month.
👉 ETFs and custodial holdings grew sharply last month, with demand adding 3.6m ounces and siphoning off liquidity.

In contrast, the LBMA OTC Trade Data shows that an average of 22.9m ounces gold changed hands every day in the 5 weeks to Aug-04. This suggests a surplus of available unallocated gold with the free-float effectively running a positive balance of 15.7m ounces.

                  ANALYZING THE FLOAT                  
                                   
                  London's free float currently covers just 1.7 days of OTC spot trading tight, but back to its long-term average after rebounding in Q2-25 as metal returned from COMEX via reversing EFP arbitrage.                  
                                   
                                   
                                   
                  Since 2020, the float has lifted from ~8% to ~14% of vault holdings. Yet this still equates to only 1 to 2 days of trading cover. Gold's liquidity is an illusion of velocity: turnover looks deep, but the true cushion of mobilizable metal remains razor-thin.                  
                                   
                                   
                                   
                                   
                  LBMA clearing data shows the float covers average daily settlements (~13.4m oz). But in a stress scenario, where investors demand physical allocation, the float would be exhausted in just three settlement days, underscoring how fragile gold's market structure remains beneath the surface.
